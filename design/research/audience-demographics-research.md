- **Video preference**: 61% prefer videos under 60 seconds
- **Content discovery**: 73% discover products primarily on social media
- **Creator trust**: 88% follow at least one influencer; 22% follow 50+
- **Sharing behavior**: Share content 67% more frequently than other age groups
- **Ad influence**: 63% say social media ads/reviews most influence purchases (Deloitte 2025)
- **Content relevance**: 56% find social media content more relevant than traditional TV (Deloitte 2025)
- **Platform shift**: 58% spend more time watching social video than streaming services
- Sources: [NuVoodoo](https://nuvoodoo.com/2025/04/04/new-data-short-form-video-explodes-in-popularity/), [Morning Consult](https://pro.morningconsult.com/analysis/gen-z-instagram-reels-tiktok-short-form-video), [Deloitte DMT 2025](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/digital-media-trends-consumption-habits-survey/2025.html)

### Millennials (born ~1981-1996)

- **Platform preference**: YouTube (90%), Facebook (89%), Instagram (81%), TikTok (69%)

- **Short-form adoption**: Well over 90% watch short-form video at least sometimes

## 7. Follow Triggers -- What Makes People Follow a Creator

### Primary Motivations (Research Synthesis)

1. **Entertainment** (41% follow for food content, 41% for entertainment content)
2. **Authenticity & Relatability** -- Relatable creators drive stronger purchasing intent than aspirational ones
3. **Personal Connection** -- 48% feel they know creators personally; 53% miss creators who don't post regularly
4. **Belonging & Identity** -- Users seek shared experiences and identity connection
5. **Information/Education** -- Gen Z actively seeks advice from influencers on media, dating, fashion, beauty

### Cross-Platform Loyalty

- 50%+ of followers follow creators across ALL their social platforms
- Followers would follow creators to new platforms if they moved
- This suggests persona loyalty transcends platform choice

### Content Topics That Drive Follows

- Food: 41%
- Entertainment: 41%
- Behind-the-scenes: High engagement trigger
- Challenges/trends: Strong with Gen Z
- Educational/how-to: Strong with Millennials

### Generational Differences

- **Gen Z**: Prioritizes authentic, unpolished content; trusts creators over brands; 88% follow at least one influencer
- **Millennials**: Values depth, storytelling, and explanatory content alongside entertainment
- **Gen X**: Drawn to practical, informational content; Facebook and YouTube are primary platforms

- Sources: [Deloitte DMT 2025](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/digital-media-trends-consumption-habits-survey/2025.html), [Morning Consult](https://pro.morningconsult.com/analysis/influencers-content-creators-evolving-power-2024), [Spiralytics](https://www.spiralytics.com/blog/content-creator-statistics-2025/)

### Funnel Stages

```
  (FYP,        (Retention    (Like,       (Commit to    (Repeat viewer,
   Explore,     decision in   comment,     creator's     advocate,
   Search,      first few     share,       content)      purchaser)

```

### Stage-by-Stage Data

**1. Discovery**
- Algorithm-driven: TikTok FYP is pure serendipity based on behavior; easiest platform to go from zero to viral
- Instagram Reels: Shown in Explore + Reels feed; influenced by existing popularity
- YouTube Shorts: Search-intent advantage; recommendations based on watch history
- 73% of Gen Z discover products on social media
- 66.6% of US consumers use social for search

**2. Watch (Retention Gate)**
- 71% of viewers decide in the first few seconds whether to continue
- Average completion: TikTok 78%, YT Shorts 73%, IG Reels 65%
- 75%+ retention = 3x algorithmic boost on YouTube

**3. Engage**
- 90-9-1 rule: 90% consume passively, 9% engage occasionally, 1% create
- Engagement measurement priority for marketers: 68% measure engagement, 65% conversion, 57% revenue

**4. Follow**
- Triggered by: entertainment value, authenticity, relatability, consistent content, personal connection
- 48% feel they know creators personally

**5. Loyalty**
- 53% miss creators who don't post regularly
- 50%+ follow across all platforms
- Social commerce funnel: post-purchase messaging, community updates, UGC drive retention

## 10. Sharer Archetypes (NYT "Psychology of Sharing" + Updates)

### The Original Six Personas (NYT/Latitude Research, 2011)

Based on ethnographies, a week-long immersive panel, and a quantitative survey of 2,500 medium-to-heavy online sharers:

| Persona      | Traits                                          | Preferred Platform           | Motivation                          |
|-------------|------------------------------------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Altruists**    | Helpful, reliable, thoughtful, connected        | Email forwarding             | Help others, be useful               |
| **Careerists**   | Intelligent, networkers, opinion-sharers        | LinkedIn                     | Professional reputation, networking  |
| **Hipsters**     | Creative, young, popular, strong identity       | Cutting-edge platforms       | Identity expression, being first     |
| **Boomerangs**   | Empowered, reactive, validation-seeking         | Facebook, X (Twitter)        | Attention, getting people talking    |
| **Connectors**   | Creative, relaxed, thoughtful, plan-initiators  | Facebook, Email              | Bringing people together             |
| **Selectives**   | Resourceful, thoughtful, careful                | Email (small groups)         | Curated sharing to trusted circles   |

### Updated Research Themes (2020-2025)

Recent systematic literature reviews (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025) identify these updated sharing drivers:

1. **Entertainment-derived usefulness** has stronger influence on sharing than information-seeking usefulness
2. **Emotional triggers**: Anger and anxiety are the strongest sharing catalysts -- users experiencing both simultaneously show highest sharing likelihood
3. **Self-referential processing**: People share content that relates to their own identity and experiences
4. **Information quality and valence**: Content credibility affects sharing willingness
5. **Theory of Planned Behavior** and **Uses & Gratifications Theory** remain the dominant frameworks

### Proposed Modern Extensions for S4 Personas

Based on the research synthesis, the original six can be augmented with behavior patterns specific to short-form video:

- **The Curator** (evolved Selective): Shares via DMs and private Stories; growing "dark social" behavior
- **The Advocate** (evolved Altruist): Shares cause-based and values-aligned content publicly
- **The Trend Rider** (evolved Hipster): Shares trending sounds, challenges, memes to signal cultural awareness
- **The Reactor** (evolved Boomerang): Uses duets, stitches, and quote-retweets as their sharing mechanism

- Sources: [Foundation Inc](https://foundationinc.co/lab/psychology-sharing-content-online/), [Engage Social](https://www.engage.social/blog/social-share/the-psychology-of-sharing-a-persona-based-approach-to-sharing-on-social-media/), [Frontiers in Psychology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1567506/full)

## 11. Lurker Behavior

### The 90-9-1 Rule (Nielsen Norman Group)

| Role         | % of Users | Behavior                              |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------------------------|
| Lurkers      | 90%       | Read/observe, never contribute        |
| Contributors | 9%        | Engage occasionally                   |
| Creators     | 1%        | Create most content                   |

- This ratio still broadly holds in 2025 for public social media
- **Exception**: In branded/private communities, up to 23% actively create content (communities feel safer than public social)

### Why Users Lurk

1. **Privacy concerns**: Don't want opinions visible; avoid controversial associations
2. **Algorithm gaming**: Deliberately avoid engaging to prevent algorithm changes to their feed
3. **Low emotional investment**: Content doesn't trigger strong enough reaction
4. **Time constraints**: Scrolling without stopping due to limited time
5. **Fear of judgment/imposter syndrome**: Especially in professional spaces
6. **Preference for observation**: Some users genuinely prefer to consume rather than participate

### What Converts Lurkers to Engagers

1. **Lower barriers**: Simple engagement options (polls, quick reactions, one-tap responses)
2. **Recognition**: Acknowledging when quiet members do participate; positive reinforcement
3. **Emotional triggers**: Misinformation, "extremely biased news," and strong emotional content activate lurkers more than neutral content
4. **Safe spaces**: Branded communities where vulnerability feels safer
5. **Direct engagement**: When brands/creators directly interact with a lurker's rare post, they return for more
6. **Role fluidity**: Users shift between lurker and creator over time -- someone may join and immediately post, then settle into lurking, or vice versa

### Implications for S4 Persona Committee

- The voting/engagement simulation must account for the fact that ~90% of real viewers are silent
- Simulated personas should include a large "silent majority" whose preferences still matter (they watch, they just don't visibly react)
- The 9% "occasional engagers" are the most realistic voting bloc -- they represent users who like/comment when something resonates
- Sources: [NN/Group](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/participation-inequality/), [Studio Thirty Four](https://www.studio-thirtyfour.com/insights/social-media-lurkers-2025), [Northeastern University](https://news.northeastern.edu/2025/04/14/social-media-lurkers-research/)

## 12. Platform-Hopping Behavior

### Cross-Platform Usage

- Short-form video users spend 2+ hours daily across MULTIPLE SFV platforms
- Creators commonly repurpose content across TikTok, Reels, and Shorts -- but performance differs significantly

### Platform Identity & Content Expectations

| Platform       | Discovery Model         | Content DNA                    | User Intent          |
|---------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| TikTok         | Algorithm-driven (FYP)  | Raw, unfiltered, trend-driven  | Entertainment, kill time |
| YouTube Shorts | Search + recommendations | Informational, educational     | Learn, discover     |
| Instagram Reels| Explore + social graph  | Aesthetic, polished, lifestyle | Aspiration, connection |

### Key Differences

- **TikTok**: Easiest to go viral from zero; purely algorithm-driven discovery; highest engagement rate (2.80%)
- **YouTube Shorts**: Search-intent advantage; niche/educational content performs well; lower engagement (0.30%) but massive reach
- **Instagram Reels**: Influenced by existing social graph and popularity; aesthetic expectations higher; mid-range engagement (0.65%)

### Same Content, Different Performance

- TikTok users rated the platform as offering more "tech affordances" than Reels or Shorts
- Simply repurposing the same video across platforms underperforms vs platform-native content
- Each ecosystem has distinct algorithms, audience behaviors, and content expectations

### User Segmentation by Platform Usage

- **TikTok-primary**: Younger, entertainment-seeking, trend-aware, lower income/education skew
- **YouTube-primary**: Broader age range, information-seeking, higher education skew
- **Instagram-primary**: Lifestyle-oriented, higher income, aesthetic-conscious, slightly older than TikTok

## Implications for S4 Persona Committee Design

### Recommended Persona Dimensions

Based on this research, each simulated audience persona in the S4 voting stage should encode:

1. **Demographics**: Age bracket, gender, income tier, education level, region
2. **Platform primary**: TikTok / YouTube Shorts / Instagram Reels
3. **Engagement archetype**: Lurker (90%) / Occasional Engager (9%) / Active Creator (1%)
4. **Sharer type**: Altruist / Careerist / Hipster / Boomerang / Connector / Selective
5. **Content preference**: Entertainment / Education / Inspiration / Social proof / Trend-riding
6. **Follow triggers**: Authenticity / Humor / Expertise / Relatability / Aspiration
7. **Generation**: Gen Z / Millennial / Gen X (each with documented behavior patterns)
8. **Regional content DNA**: US / UK / SEA / LATAM / MENA (each with distinct preferences)
9. **Session behavior**: Binge-watcher / Quick-checker / Bedtime scroller / Lunch-break viewer
10. **Retention threshold**: First-second judge / Patient viewer / Completion-oriented

### Realistic Engagement Distribution

A committee of N simulated personas should roughly follow:
- ~60-70% passive watchers (view but don't vote/react)
- ~20-30% occasional engagers (vote on content that resonates with their archetype)
- ~5-10% active engagers (comment, share, strong opinions)

This mirrors real platform engagement patterns and prevents the simulation from being unrealistically positive.
